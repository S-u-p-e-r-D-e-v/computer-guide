What is a Computer?

A computer is an electronic device that accepts input, processes data, stores information, and produces output. It follows instructions called programs to perform tasks efficiently.

Computer
├── Input: Keyboard, Mouse, Microphone, Scanner
├── Processing: CPU (Central Processing Unit)
├── Storage: Hard Drives, SSD
├── Output: Monitor, Printer, Speakers
└── Communication: Network Cards, Wi-Fi Adapters

Modern computers are everywhere – from tiny smartwatches to massive data centers.

Main Components

  • CPU (Central Processing Unit): The brain of the computer that performs calculations and runs programs.
  • RAM (Random Access Memory): Temporary memory for quick data access by the CPU while running programs.
  • Storage (SSD/HDD): Long-term memory where your files, programs, and operating system live.
  • GPU (Graphics Processing Unit): Specialized processor to render images, videos, and 3D graphics smoothly.
  • Motherboard: The main circuit board connecting all components.
  • Power Supply Unit (PSU): Converts electricity from your outlet to usable power for your computer parts.
  • Cooling System: Fans, heat sinks, or liquid coolers that keep components from overheating.
  • Input Devices: Keyboard, mouse, microphone, and more for user interaction.
  • Output Devices: Monitor, speakers, printer that display or play the computer’s response.

Each component plays a specific role to make your computer work smoothly.

Software vs Hardware

Hardware is the physical parts of your computer you can touch. Examples include the keyboard, mouse, hard drive, and screen.

Software consists of the programs and instructions that tell the hardware what to do. Examples: Operating systems (Windows, macOS, Linux), apps like browsers (Chrome, Firefox), games, and utilities.

For instance, the YouTube app (software) runs on your phone’s hardware.

Together, software and hardware work as a system.

What is Task Manager?

The Task Manager is a tool that shows you all the programs and background processes running on your computer. It provides information about their CPU, memory, disk, and network usage.

You can open it quickly by pressing Ctrl + Shift + Esc on Windows.

It helps you:

  • See which programs are slowing down your PC.
  • End unresponsive applications.
  • Monitor system performance.

What is Hertz (Hz)?

  • CPU Clock Speed: Measured in Gigahertz (GHz), showing how many billions of cycles the CPU can perform every second.
  • RAM Speed: Measured in Megahertz (MHz), indicating how fast the memory can be read or written.
  • Monitor Refresh Rate: Measured in Hertz (Hz), indicating how many times per second the screen updates. Higher refresh rates mean smoother visuals.

Higher numbers usually mean better performance, but other factors also matter.

Networking Basics

  • Modem: Device that connects your home network to the internet service provider (ISP).
  • Router: Distributes internet connection to multiple devices within your network, wired or wirelessly.
  • Wi-Fi: Wireless network connection, convenient but sometimes slower or less stable than wired.
  • Ethernet: Wired network connection, usually faster and more reliable than Wi-Fi.
  • IP Address: A unique number assigned to each device on a network, like a street address for sending data.
  • DNS (Domain Name System): Translates website names (like google.com) into IP addresses your computer can understand.

What is a Server?

A server is a computer or system that provides resources, data, services, or programs to other computers, called clients, over a network. Common types include:

  • Web Server: Hosts websites and delivers webpages to your browser.
  • File Server: Stores and manages files that multiple users can access.
  • Game Server: Hosts multiplayer games online.
  • Mail Server: Handles sending and receiving emails.

You can even repurpose an old PC as a personal server at home.

Why Computers Slow Down

  • Too many apps and background processes running at once.
  • Insufficient RAM for your tasks.
  • Old or nearly full storage drives.
  • Overheating due to dust build-up or failing cooling.
  • Malware or viruses.
  • Outdated or fragmented software and drivers.

Regular maintenance like deleting unnecessary files, updating software, and running antivirus scans can keep your PC speedy.

Basic Computer Security

Keeping your computer safe from threats is essential. Here are some tips:

  • Use strong, unique passwords for your accounts.
  • Keep your operating system and software updated regularly.
  • Install and maintain reputable antivirus and anti-malware programs.
  • Be cautious with email attachments and unknown downloads.
  • Backup your important files regularly.
  • Use firewalls and secure your Wi-Fi with a strong password.
  • Learn to recognize phishing scams and suspicious links.

A Brief History of the Internet

The internet began as a U.S. government project in the late 1960s called ARPANET, aimed at connecting computers for communication. It expanded in the 1980s and 1990s to universities and businesses, growing into the global network we use today.

Key milestones include:

  • 1989: Tim Berners-Lee invents the World Wide Web.
  • 1993: First graphical web browser, Mosaic, popularizes internet use.
  • 2000s: Broadband and Wi-Fi become widespread.
  • Today: Billions of devices connected globally, enabling streaming, social media, cloud computing, and more.

Understanding the internet's history helps appreciate how computers connect and share information worldwide.
